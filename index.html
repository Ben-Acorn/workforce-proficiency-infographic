A Researcher's Guide to Workforce Proficiency Frameworks

An analysis of models for developing tasks, skills, and capability frameworks. This overview examines their structure, assumptions, strengths, and best-use cases.

How to Categorise the Models

The 10 models aren't mutually exclusive; they represent different philosophies. We can group them into five key 'families' based on their core focus, from component parts to holistic development.

Component & Behavioural Models

These focus on deconstructing performance. Component models (KSA, Know-Be-Do) identify the 'ingredients' of a person, while Behavioural models (Competency, Critical Incident) focus on observable actions.

Task & Developmental Models

This pair focuses on progression. Task/Skill models (Skill-Based, Miller's) track mastery of specific procedures. Developmental models (Dreyfus, Bloom's) track the *cognitive* journey from novice to expert.

Organisational & System Models

These models zoom out to look at the entire system. Capability frameworks bundle skills into broader organisational needs, while Maturity models assess the process of the team or system itself, not just the individual.

Detailed Framework Analysis

1. KSA / KSAO Models

Work is a mix of what people know, can do, and bring as attributes.

Strengths

  • Comprehensive and foundational.
  • Good for job analysis and selection.

Weaknesses

  • Components can blur (e.g., Ability vs. Skill).
  • Can become a static "laundry list".

Best Use Case

Defining role requirements for recruitment and selection.

2. Know–Be–Do Framework

Performance blends cognition (Know), identity (Be), and action (Do).

Strengths

  • Holistic; integrates values and mindset.
  • Strong for leadership development.

Weaknesses

  • 'Be' element is hard to standardise.
  • Less suited for purely technical domains.

Best Use Case

Leadership and professional identity development programs.

3. Competency Frameworks

Behaviours are the core unit of measurement.

Strengths

  • Common language for performance.
  • Directly observable and measurable.

Weaknesses

  • Can become too generic ("cookie-cutter").
  • High maintenance; behaviours can be low-utility.

Best Use Case

Large-scale performance management and HR systems.

4. Skill/Task-Based Frameworks

Skills are discrete, trainable, and measurable units.

Strengths

  • Very clear and specific.
  • Excellent for training and development.

Weaknesses

  • Brittle; skills and tasks change quickly.
  • Can miss "soft skills" or judgement.

Best Use Case

Technical training, onboarding, and skills-gap analysis.

5. Cognitive Taxonomies (Bloom/SOLO)

Proficiency is measured by cognitive complexity.

Strengths

  • Strong, validated model for learning.
  • Forces clarity on cognitive levels.

Weaknesses

  • Not built for workplace performance.
  • Weak on procedural or interpersonal skills.

Best Use Case

Designing learning outcomes and educational curricula.

6. Miller's Pyramid

Maps progression from knowledge to real-world action.

Strengths

  • Clear, logical, and actionable progression.
  • Excellent for assessing readiness.

Weaknesses

  • Very specific to clinical/regulated fields.
  • Less useful for broad mixed-skill domains.

Best Use Case

Regulated professions (medicine, law, aviation) for assessment.

7. Dreyfus Novice–Expert Model

Proficiency is a journey from rules to intuition.

Strengths

  • Accurately describes high-level expertise.
  • Values judgement and intuition.

Weaknesses

  • Highly qualitative and hard to measure.
  • Difficult to operationalise at scale.

Best Use Case

Mentoring programs, talent development for senior roles.

8. Capability Frameworks (Modern)

Capabilities are integrated bundles of KSA, tools, and context.

Strengths

  • Holistic and context-aware.
  • Future-focused and strategic.

Weaknesses

  • Can become overly abstract ("strategic thinking").
  • Requires clear performance evidence to anchor.

Best Use Case

Strategic workforce planning and organisational design.

9. Maturity Models

Describes system maturity, not individual performance.

Strengths

  • Excellent for process improvement.
  • Clear path for team/system development.

Weaknesses

  • Often incorrectly applied to individuals.
  • This is a category error for personal skills.

Best Use Case

Assessing team, process, or organisational (e.g., data analytics) maturity.

10. Behavioural/Critical Incident

Generates indicators by analysing high-impact events.

Strengths

  • Superior validity and relevance.
  • Directly tied to proven performance.

Weaknesses

  • Extremely labour-intensive to create.
  • Requires significant research and analysis.

Best Use Case

Developing high-stakes selection tools or bespoke competency models.
